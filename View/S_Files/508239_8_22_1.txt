<p><strong>c</strong>&nbsp;(<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/help:ipa_for_english">/ˈsiː/</a>, as in&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/c">the letter&nbsp;<em>c</em></a>) is a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/general-purpose_language">general-purpose</a>,&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/imperative_programming">imperative</a>&nbsp;computer&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/programming_language">programming language</a>, supporting&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/structured_programming">structured programming</a>,&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/lexical_variable_scope">lexical variable scope</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/recursion_(computer_science)">recursion</a>, while a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/static_type_system">static type system</a>&nbsp;prevents many unintended operations. by design, c provides constructs that map efficiently to typical&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/machine_instruction">machine instructions</a>, and therefore it has found lasting use in applications that had formerly been coded in&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/@***@embly_language">@***@embly language</a>, including&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/operating_system">operating systems</a>, as well as various&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/application_software">application software</a>&nbsp;for computers ranging from&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/supercomputer">supercomputers</a>&nbsp;to&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/embedded_system">embedded systems</a>.</p>
